4.4: Congruence of Figures

Two figures are congruent if every corresponding side and every corresponding angle are the same from one figure to the next. The symbol for congruence is ≅.

In these figures, ∆CDE ≅ ∆EQR.

With the exception of dilation, the transformations we have learned are rigid transformations, meaning that the sides remain unchanged and the angles remain unchanged during the transformation. This means that we can use the rigid transformations—reflection, rotation, and translation—to show that two figures are congruent.

Example 1

Determine if the two figures are congruent using transformations.

First, since one figure is turned a different way than the other, we can rotate the triangle ABC 90 degrees clockwise.

Finally we translate triangle ABC up 1 and right 1, and then we have triangle ABC exactly lining up with triangle DEF. Thus, the two figures are congruent.

Example 2

Determine if the two figures are congruent using transformations.

One figure is a mirror for the other, so we reflect triangle ABC across the y-axis.

Then we translate the triangle down 4 so we line up pint A with point D. However, we notice that when we do so, the other points do not line up.

Thus, these two triangles are not congruent.
