Simplify Roots

The easiest way to simplify a root is to look for a perfect square in a square root or perfect cube in a cube root. For example, if we want to find the square root of 25, we know that 5 multiplied by itself is 25; the square root of 25 equals 5.

There are times when we try to simplify a radical and it is not a perfect square. What do we do then? We prime factor. A prime number is one that cannot be split into factors other than itself and one. Often we use a tree diagram to show our factoring. Let’s look at a number and break it up into its prime factors.

When there are pairs of factors (perfect squares) within the expression, they can be moved out from under the radical sign. In this case, the equation would be changed from the square root of 5 times 3 times 3 to 3 times the square root of 5.

Follow the same steps to simplify a cube root, but pull out three of a kind rather than a pair. For example, the factors in the cube root of 72 are 8 times 9, and 8 is a perfect cube with factors of 2, 2, and 2. Then the 9 can be factored to 3 and 3, resulting in prime factors of 2, 2, 2, 3, and 3. The number representing the perfect cube (2) can be pulled out of the radical sign. The equation would be 2 times the cube root of 3 times 3, or 2 times the cube root of 9.
