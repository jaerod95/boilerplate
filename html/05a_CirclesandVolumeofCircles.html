Circles and Volume of Cylinders

In order to find the volume of things that are round, we actually have to begin with a circle.

Formula for the area of a circle:

A = πr2

In this formula, A represents the area of the circle, π (pi) is a number that is approximately 3.14, and r is the radius of the circle. The radius of the circle is the distance from the center of the circle to the outside. For example, this circle has a radius of 7.8 km.

Example 1

Find the area of a circle with a radius of 4. Round your answer to the nearest hundredth decimal place. This is most easily done using the formul we were given. First we take the formula:

A = πr2

Then we put in the values of r and an approximate value of π.

A = (3.14)(4)2
A = (3.14)(16)
A = 50.24

Now that we can find the area of a circle, we can find the volume of a cylinder.

Imagine for a minute that a cylinder is simply a series of circles that are all the same size, stacked on top of each other.

Then all we would need to do to find the volume is to multiply the area of one of the circles times the thickness of the circles.

This, in fact, is the way that we will find most of our volumes; we will simply take the base of the shape, which is a circle in the case of the cylinder, and then multiply by its height. Here is the formula for the volume of a cylinder:

V = πr2h

In this formula, V is the volume, 3.14 is an approximation for π, r is the radius of the circle that is on the base of the cylinder, and h is the height of the cylinder.

Example 2

To find the volume of the cylinder with a base that has a radius of 3 cm. and a height of 4 cm, we start with the formula for the volume of the cylinder:

V = πr2h

Then we plug the values into the formula:

V = πr2h
V = (3.14)(3)24
V = 3.14(36)
V = 113.04 cm3

Notice that the answer is in cm3 because we are multiplying centimeters by itself three times. Answers to volume problems are always in cubic dimensions, implying tiny little cubes that make up the volume.

Example 3

Find the volume of the cylinder shown.
