Volume of Cones

When we look at the volume of a one, we compare it first to a cylinder.

In a cylinder, the size of the circle at the base is constant all the way up the length of the cylinder. In a cone, the circle at the base gets smaller and smaller until it comes to a point. In this way, the volume is quite a bit smaller for a cone, even though the height and the base may be identical to that of the cylinder. In fact, the volume is exactly one-third of that of the cylinder.

We then use that to calculate the volume of the cone.

Formula for the volume of a cone:

V = (1/3)πr2h

In this formula, V is the volume of the cone, 3.14 is an approximate value for π, r is the radius of the circle at the base of the cone, and h is the perpendicular height from the base of the cone to the tip of cone.

Example 1

Find the volume of a cone with a height of 10 and a radius of 3. Round your answer to the nearest hundredth, and use 3.14 as the value for π.

First take the formula:

V = (1/3)πr2h

Then plug the values into the formula:

V = (1/3)πr2h
V = (1/3)(3.14)d3(10)
V = 94.2
