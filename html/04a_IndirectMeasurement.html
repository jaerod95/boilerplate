Indirect Measurement

In the last unit, we looked at dilations using a scale factor to make a smaller or larger figure.

Notice that this figure looks exactly the same—the angles are the same, and only the sides are larger or smaller—and yet the sides are in proportion to each other. When this occurs, we call the two figures similar.

Since dilation occurs using a scale factor, we can actually use this scale factor to solve equations or to set up a proportion—which allows us to find the length of a side that is missing.

In a triangle, all the angles add to 180 degrees. If we know two angles of one triangle, we can figure out the other angle by using subtraction. For example, if a triangle has angles of 90 degrees and 27 degrees, then by subtraction 180 − 90 − 27 = 63 degrees.

If we have two triangles that have two sets of equal angles, then their other angle must also be equal. Then, if all their angles are equal, the two triangles are similar. We call this Angle-Angle (AA) Similarity.

In the first triangle, the measure of one of the angles is 37 degrees, and the other is a right angle, which is 90 degrees. Thus, since all triangles have measure of 180 degrees, 180 − 90 − 37 = 53 degrees. This matches the number of degrees in the other triangle, and since the two triangles both have 90-degree angles, the two triangles are similar using AA similarity.
