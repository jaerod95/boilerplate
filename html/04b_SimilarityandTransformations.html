Similarity and Transformation

Two figures are similar if they have the same angle measures and the lengths of the sides are proportional.

We can show that two figures are similar by using the transformations that we have already studied. In order for the two figures to be similar, there must be a dilation—either smaller or larger—and then some other type of transformation, such as rotation, translation, or reflection.

Example 1

Determine if the two triangles are similar using transformations.

In the figure, segment ED is twice as long as BA, segment EF is twice as long as BC, and DF is twice as long as AC, so we know a dilation of 2 occurred. When we perform the dilation on triangle ABC, we can translate the figure down 3 and left 3 to have the triangles line up.

Example 2

Determine if the two figures are similar using tranformations.

First we see that EFGH is a mirror image of ABCD, so we reflect ABCD about the x-axis. We also see that AB has a length of 4 and EF has a length of 10. This is a dilation of 2.5 times. When we do both the reflection and the dilation, we see that the two figures are the same, so the figures are similar.
